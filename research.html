Recommendations

  • Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction (Sutton & Barto)
    My go-to RL textbook. I like how it builds intuition step by step ‚Äì returns, value functions, TD methods ‚Äì with small examples instead of jumping straight into deep RL.
  • Convex Optimization (Boyd & Vandenberghe)
    I enjoy the geometric view of optimization here ‚Äì why problems are convex, how constraints change the shape, and how this connects to things like regression and SVMs. Feels very close to how we actually model real problems.
  • Introduction to Applied Linear Algebra (Boyd & Vandenberghe)
    Super clean introduction to vectors, matrices, and least squares, with lots of computational thinking. I like it as a bridge between theory and ‚Äúhow you would actually code this in practice.‚Äù
  • Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning: Foundations and Modern Approaches
    A big-picture book on agents that learn and interact together. I like how it mixes classic game-theoretic ideas (cooperation, competition) with modern deep RL tricks, without getting lost in notation.

Already read / working through

  • Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms (Schulman et al., 2017)
    Paper that introduces PPO ‚Äì a simple clipping trick that makes policy-gradient updates much more stable. I like it because it gives a very practical recipe: collect rollouts, do several epochs of clipped updates, and you already get strong results on MuJoCo and Atari without crazy tuning.
  • Domain Randomization for Transferring Deep Neural Networks from Simulation to the Real World (Tobin et al., 2017)
    Idea: instead of making the simulator perfectly realistic, you deliberately randomize textures, lighting, and camera parameters so much that the network learns to be robust. I like this as a very ‚Äúengineering‚Äù way to reduce the sim-to-real gap for robotic perception.
